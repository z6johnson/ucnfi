# UCNFI Steering Committee Pass 1 Summary

Twenty-three members enriched. This document reads the records together rather than one at a time, and surfaces the patterns most likely to shape how working groups are formed and how the kickoff gets framed.

## What the committee actually looks like

The committee has 23 members across five sectors: 16 UC campus faculty and administrators, 2 UCOP (Williams, Kirschner), 2 UC Health (Han, Murray), 2 national lab (Hagberg, Neely), and 1 nonprofit (counted as Crittenden's CITRIS leadership). Of those 23, the AI-relationship breakdown is roughly: 12 governance/policy, 9 deployers, 7 builders/researchers, 6 critics/scholars, and 3 user representatives. That's a good distribution. The committee is not stacked toward any one of those orientations.

The strongest expertise concentrations, in descending order, are:

- Higher education strategy and innovation (11)
- AI ethics and algorithmic accountability (10)
- AI/ML research and development (8)
- Embedded systems and computing infrastructure (8)
- Public-sector AI policy and procurement (7)
- Digital humanities and critical technology studies (5)

The thinnest are health AI and clinical deployment (1, Murray), mathematics and formal methods (1, Hagberg), health data governance (2), and academic shared governance (3).

## Clusters worth naming

A few recurring groupings showed up across batches that aren't visible from any single record.

**The infrastructure cluster.** Williams, Gupta, Hagberg, Neely, Dugan, Vigna. Six people who together can speak to: UC system IT, the largest campus computing/data school, two of the three NNSA Tri-Lab supercomputing programs (LANL and LLNL — El Capitan is the world's most powerful supercomputer), the smallest-campus IT perspective, and AI-cybersecurity at NSF Institute scale. This is a working group's worth of infrastructure capacity already at the table. OA-4 should not have to recruit beyond the committee.

**The critical AI bench.** Noble, Raley, Milburn, Zimmer. Four humanities scholars whose work is explicitly about AI, not just adjacent to it. Three of them have institutional positions that matter: Noble (UCLA Center on Resilience & Digital Justice, MacArthur Fellow), Milburn (UC Davis CAIEF, NEH-funded national Humanities Research Center on AI with 26 affiliated faculty), Raley (co-author of the field-naming "Critical AI: A Field in Formation" piece). Zimmer is the lightest of the four and probably best as a contributor under one of the others. **CAIEF in particular is a working partner-of-opportunity** — an existing NEH-funded national center built for exactly the kind of work OA-1 wants to do.

**The health voice is just two people.** Han (UC Health systemwide chief health data officer) and Murray (UCSF chief health AI officer, now VP-level). For a UC system that includes six health systems, two health voices on a 23-person committee is structurally light. OA-8 will work because they are both very strong, but it would not survive losing either of them.

**Three chancellors, plus several senior administrators, plus the senate chair.** Khosla (UCSD), Hu (UCR), and arguably also Williams as VP at UCOP. Plus Palazoglu as Senate Chair (a non-administrator but with comparable influence in shared-governance terms). Plus Crittenden as CITRIS director. Plus Gupta as a founding dean. The committee is unusually senior-administrator-heavy. That's a feature for getting things done; it could become a liability if the committee starts producing decisions before working groups have had time to form.

**The previous AI Council's institutional memory.** Three current Steering Committee members served on the predecessor body: Crittenden (Student Experience subcommittee co-chair), Moe (Risk + Transparency subcommittee co-chair), and Han (member). The new committee should not relitigate ground the previous body covered. These three are the institutional memory.

## Committee-level corrections to surface before kickoff

These are factual issues with the deck appendix that should be fixed in committee-facing materials. None are major, but they will be visible.

- **Arkilic name spelling.** Roster has "Errol Arkilich"; every UCI source uses "Arkilic" (no h). Confirmed against UCI official appointment announcement, BAI press releases, and his MIT PhD record.
- **Hagberg division title.** Roster lists "Computing and Artificial Intelligence Division Lead" at LANL. Public record consistently uses "Computer, Computational, and Statistical Sciences Division Leader" (Wikipedia, LANL bio, SIAM 2023 fellow citation, Council on Competitiveness). Either LANL renamed the division or the deck used aspirational language. Worth confirming before publication.
- **Williams unit name.** Roster's deck appendix uses one phrasing; current UCOP usage is "Vice President of Digital Innovation and Technology" (DigIT, formerly ITS). Worth consistent usage in committee materials.
- **Murray's title.** Roster says "Chief Health AI Officer, UCSF." She was promoted to VP and Chief Health AI Officer at UCSF in 2023. The VP-level title is current.
- **Moe's title.** Roster says "Associate Vice Provost, UC Online." His public title transitioned from Executive Director to Associate Vice Provost recently — confirm which version he uses for committee materials.
- **Noble's center name.** UCLA School of Education & Information Studies and gender.ucla.edu still use "Center on Race & Digital Justice." Her personal site (and Pande Lectures bio) uses "Center on Resilience & Digital Justice." Personal site is likely most current. Flag for her to confirm.
- **Raley's rank.** Some older UCSB sources say "Associate Professor." Recent UCSB English Department, Mind and Machine Intelligence, and Comparative Literature pages list as "Professor." Used current title.
- **Kirschner's primary affiliation framing.** Her paid role is President of Meyer Family Philanthropies. Her committee-relevant role is Special Advisor to the UC President. Current draft uses Meyer; for committee-facing materials the Special Advisor framing may be more appropriate. Worth a deliberate decision.
- **Han's titles.** She is both Chief Health Data Officer at UC Health and Executive Director of CDI2. Current draft lists both. Confirm which to lead with in committee materials.

## Cross-cutting flags

A few things that don't fit a single member but are worth carrying forward.

**The profiles doc systematically undersells members.** This is the most important pattern from Pass 1. The deck appendix and the source profiles consistently understate AI-specific positions, recent recognition, and current institutional roles. Specific examples: Khosla's AI/ML credentials (AAAI Fellow, founded CMU CyLab); Murray's HEAAL framework co-authorship; Tassio's role as past AI Council co-chair; Noble's MacArthur Fellowship and non-profit; Milburn's CAIEF directorship; Raley's role co-naming the Critical AI field; Crittenden's prior Student Experience subcommittee leadership. **The committee is more credentialed than the deck suggests.** The kickoff should not rely on the deck appendix as the introduction-to-committee document. A revised member-facing one-pager based on these enriched records would land better.

**The student records are thin by design and that's correct.** Both Martin and Zambani have minimal public footprint. The standard expertise-tag framing fits faculty and admin members but doesn't fit students well — their value is lived experience and current academic perspective rather than tagged expertise. The survey instrument should be designed differently for student members.

**Vigna has a concurrent industry role at Broadcom.** Senior Director, Threat Analysis Unit. This is unusual in committee composition. It's an asset for OA-2 (he can speak credibly about industry-academic interface) but should be flagged for transparency on any work that touches procurement, vendor evaluation, or partnership selection.

**Khosla and Gupta are both at UCSD.** They are complementary rather than duplicative — Khosla as chancellor, Gupta as the founding dean of the school explicitly built around AI. But UCSD ends up with the most committee weight of any campus when you count Han (Chief Health Data Officer for UC Health, but UCSD is her institutional base), Zambani (UCSD undergrad), and Frank Wuerthwein adjacent through SDSC oversight. Worth knowing for working group composition so the committee doesn't become UCSD-shaped without intent.

## Schema and taxonomy revisions to consider before survey design

Pass 1 surfaced four small things worth fixing before we build the survey instrument against the schema.

1. **`governance_orientation` enum needs a value for civil-society / public-interest advocacy.** Noble and Milburn both do significant work in that space — engaging civil rights organizations, advisory boards, foundations, NEH partnerships — that doesn't quite fit `state_policy` (too narrow) or `research_integrity` (too internal). Recommended addition: `public_interest_advocacy`.

2. **`ai_relationship` could use a value for affected populations.** `user_representative` is the closest current fit for the student members, but it doesn't quite capture that they are members of the population AI policy will affect rather than just users of AI tools. Recommended consideration: `affected_population` or similar.

3. **The profile-doc-vs-public-record discrepancy pattern.** This recurred enough that a `profile_vs_public_discrepancy` field on the schema (or a more structured `needs_attention` taxonomy) might help track which corrections still need to flow back into committee materials.

4. **The expertise tag taxonomy could probably collapse a few areas.** Network and connectivity infrastructure (4 members, all medium-confidence, all overlapping with embedded systems) might fold into the broader infrastructure tag. Health AI (1) and health data governance (2) could potentially collapse into a single health AI/data tag. The decisions here matter for survey design — too many tags fragments the picture, too few loses precision.

## Where this lands

These are not obvious calls and worth a short conversation before moving into Pass 2.
