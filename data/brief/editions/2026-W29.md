## item-1 — California AB 2392 clears Assembly 77-0, requesting UC join a joint GenAI procurement and training working group before deploying GenAI systems to campus populations

### What happened
AB 2392, an Ed Tech bill approved by the California Assembly 77-0 on May 26 and now advancing through the Senate, would require CCC and CSU — and request UC — to convene a joint working group to produce procurement standards and training recommendations before providing any generative AI system to students, faculty, or staff, with a reporting deadline of January 1, 2028. The bill is currently active in the California Legislature.

### Why it matters to UC
UC has no systemwide enterprise AI platform and campuses have deployed GenAI under widely varying local procurement processes — ZotGPT at UCI, TritonGPT at UCSD, ChatGPT Enterprise at UCLA, vendor-only tools at UC Merced, and others — with no shared procurement standards framework. If AB 2392 is enacted as written, the 'request' directed at UC creates political pressure to participate in an intersegmental working group whose recommendations could effectively constrain how UC procures and deploys GenAI systemwide, in an area where UC currently has risk assessment guidance but no systemwide procurement standard.

### For the committee
Should UC proactively designate a working group lead and engage the AB 2392 process now to shape the procurement standards, or wait to assess the final statutory language before committing resources?

## item-4 — DHS final rule converting F, J, and I visa admissions to fixed-period status creates new administrative compliance obligations UC must assess for AI-assisted international student tracking

### What happened
A DHS final rule published July 17, 2026 amends regulations governing F (academic student), J (exchange visitor), and I (foreign media) nonimmigrant classifications, changing admission from 'duration of status' to a fixed time period with new extension-of-stay requirements. The rule takes effect under the Federal Register publication and imposes new DHS oversight and verification obligations on host institutions.

### Why it matters to UC
UC enrolls tens of thousands of F and J visa holders across all ten campuses. The shift from duration-of-status to fixed-period admission creates new administrative workflows — tracking expiration dates, managing extensions, and reporting compliance — that campuses will likely address in part through automated or AI-assisted systems. UC's OGC AI Alert prohibits sharing personal information with AI tools without a fully executed agreement, and UC's data classification framework flags student personal data as P3 or higher. Any AI-assisted visa compliance tooling would need to clear UC's risk assessment process, which currently covers administrative settings but has not been tested against immigration data in a federal compliance context.

### For the committee
Before campuses deploy AI tools to manage the new fixed-period F/J visa tracking requirements, should UCOP issue proactive guidance clarifying which data classification tiers apply and whether existing vendor agreements cover this use case?

## item-5 — National Academies report co-authored by committee member Vigna finds AI will sharply elevate near-term cybersecurity risk; UC has no systemwide AI-specific cybersecurity governance layer

### What happened
A National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine rapid expert consultation released June 24, 2026 — 'Implications of Recent Advancements in Artificial Intelligence for Cybersecurity' — concludes that AI will fundamentally reshape the cybersecurity landscape, elevating near-term risks while offering long-run defensive potential with sustained investment. Committee member Giovanni Vigna, director of the NSF ACTION AI Institute at UCSB, is a named contributor and is quoted in multiple press outlets stating that 'cybersecurity will need to improve rapidly to meet this challenge' and that 'with investment and collaboration, AI could facilitate a stronger approach to cybersecurity that may shift the advantage to the defenders.'

### Why it matters to UC
UC's security posture for AI is anchored to IS-3 and the OGC AI Alert's PII prohibitions and data classification requirements, but UC has no systemwide AI-specific cybersecurity governance layer — no policy addressing AI-enabled threats to UC infrastructure, AI-assisted red-teaming, or agentic AI security risks. The National Academies report's call for institutional investment and coordination maps directly onto a gap in UC's governance architecture that the steering committee is positioned to address. Vigna's institutional role at UCSB's ACTION Institute gives UC a credentialed internal expert resource the committee has not formally activated.

### For the committee
Should the committee task Vigna with translating the National Academies findings into a UC-specific AI cybersecurity risk brief, and does UCOP need to extend the existing IS-3/AI Alert framework to address AI-enabled threats — not just AI tool misuse?
