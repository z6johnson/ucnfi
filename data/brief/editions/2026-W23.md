## item-1 — Four California AI bills cross chambers, covering healthcare AI, chatbots, and workplace surveillance tools UC already deploys.

### What happened
Eight California bills crossed chambers during the week of June 1, 2026. Four with direct UC relevance passed the Assembly: AB 2575 (health care services AI), AB 1609 (customer service chatbots), AB 2023 (companion chatbots and children's safety), and AB 1883 (workplace surveillance tools). If enacted, these bills would impose new disclosure, oversight, or procurement obligations on AI tools UC campuses and health systems currently operate.

### Why it matters to UC
UC has a systemwide legal guidance document (OGC AI Alert) and a risk assessment framework that reference IS-3 and existing state law, but the applicable law mapping has not been updated to reflect AB 2575, AB 1609, AB 2023, or AB 1883 — all of which postdate the June 2024 legal task force chart. UC Health deploys clinical AI across six health systems under a federated governance model with no single policy that would satisfy a new state healthcare AI mandate uniformly. At the campus level, at least four campuses (UCLA, UCI, UCSD, UCR) run enterprise chatbot or AI-assistant tools that could fall under AB 1609 or AB 2023 disclosure requirements, and none has a systemwide disclosure standard for those tools.

### For the committee
Should UCOP's Office of General Counsel be tasked now to map AB 2575, AB 1609, AB 2023, and AB 1883 against current UC deployments and flag which tools or health-system uses would require remediation if any of the four bills is signed?

## item-2 — Enterprise AI pricing is rising sharply and unpredictably, threatening UC's ability to sustain current multi-campus tool contracts.

### What happened
An analysis published May 29, 2026 documents that hyperscalers are investing nearly $1 trillion annually in AI infrastructure, driving enterprise AI pricing upward across the market. Anthropic shifted to usage-based pricing causing immediate cost spikes for enterprise customers; Gemini alternatives offer significantly lower costs. CIOs at peer enterprises are already reconsidering AI deployment scope due to budget pressure.

### Why it matters to UC
UC has no systemwide enterprise AI platform — campuses have independently adopted their own: vendor contracts for ChatGPT Enterprise (UCLA, UCSF) and Google Gemini Enterprise (UCR), UCI's customized ZotGPT suite, and UCSD's TritonGPT, which UC San Diego built and operates in-house on San Diego Supercomputer Center infrastructure (and has since licensed to UC Berkeley, SDSU, and others) rather than contracting from a vendor. This fragmentation means commercial-tool pricing volatility hits each contracting campus separately with no consolidated negotiating position, while self-hosted platforms like TritonGPT sit largely outside that exposure. The UC Davis AI Council explicitly flagged the 'lack of an effective and financially accessible UC-wide genAI tool' as a problem in its February 2025 draft report and estimated AggieAI at $25K/year in token costs, a figure that would change materially under usage-based repricing. There is no systemwide AI infrastructure budget or pooled procurement mechanism that the baseline records.

### For the committee
Does the committee want to recommend a systemwide AI procurement working group with delegated negotiating authority — or explicitly defer to campus-level contracting — before the next major vendor contract renewal cycle?

## item-3 — NIST dissolved the AI Safety Institute Consortium (AISIC), removing the federal standards body UC policy references as a governing framework anchor.

### What happened
A Federal Register notice published May 29, 2026 announced that NIST is reconstituting or winding down the Artificial Intelligence Safety Institute Consortium (AISIC), which had brought together more than 280 organizations since November 2023 to develop empirically backed AI guidelines and standards. The notice signals a material change to the federal AI standards infrastructure that universities and other institutions have been citing in their governance frameworks.

### Why it matters to UC
UC's systemwide applicable law and policy mapping explicitly references the NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF) and its GenAI Profile as governing standards for UC AI use. UCLA's AI Use and Recommendation Guide is described as aligned with NIST AI RMF. UCSF's Trustworthy AI framework cites NIST AI RMF. If AISIC — the primary body producing empirical guidance under the RMF — is dissolved or restructured, UC's cited standards anchor becomes uncertain without a designated replacement. UC has no recorded position on what standard to apply if NIST AI RMF guidance is no longer actively maintained.

### For the committee
Should UC identify an alternative or supplementary standards reference (e.g., ISO 42001, EU AI Act conformance frameworks, CHAI standards) in the event that NIST AI RMF guidance is no longer updated, and who owns that determination systemwide?

## item-4 — FDA extended comment period on AI-enabled clinical trial optimization pilot, opening a submission window directly relevant to UC Health research programs.

### What happened
The FDA published a Federal Register notice on May 28, 2026 extending the comment period for its AI-Enabled Optimization of Early-Phase Clinical Trials Pilot Program Request for Information, which originally appeared April 29, 2026. The pilot program would assess how AI can optimize early-phase clinical trial design and operations. The extension gives institutions additional time to submit comments shaping how FDA will regulate AI in clinical research.

### Why it matters to UC
UC operates six health systems that run early-phase clinical trials; UC Davis Health, UCSF, UCSD Health, and UCLA Health all have documented clinical AI deployments and governance structures. The VALID AI initiative — which spans all six UC health systems — is directly positioned on AI in clinical and research settings. However, UC has no recorded systemwide policy on AI in sponsored research or clinical trials that would constitute a unified institutional position for an FDA comment submission. UCSD has a research AI policy through its Senate-Admin workgroup; UCSF has IRB guidance on LLMs in human subjects research; but there is no cross-system coordination mechanism documented for responding to federal regulatory proceedings on clinical AI.

### For the committee
Should UC Health, through CDI2 or the Health AI Governance Forum, coordinate a single UC system comment to the FDA pilot RFI before the extended deadline, and is there a standing mechanism to do that or does one need to be created?

## item-5 — UCR Chancellor announces UC–CSU AI education collaboration and RAISE@UCR Institute, adding an inter-system workforce dimension the committee has no current framework to address.

### What happened
Chancellor S. Jack Hu's May 29, 2026 Chancellor's Update announced a new collaboration between UC Riverside and the California State University system focused on expanding AI education and workforce preparation, alongside developments at the RAISE@UCR AI Institute and SoCal OASIS Park. This is a cross-system partnership between UC and CSU — a relationship governed at the President and Chancellor level — with an explicit AI workforce mandate.

### Why it matters to UC
UC has a systemwide AI Council and a new AI Steering Committee, but neither the baseline nor any campus document records a UC policy or framework for AI education partnerships with other California public higher education systems. The UC AI Council's 2025 priorities reference amplifying state and federal AI initiatives for workforce upskilling, but there is no documented mechanism for coordinating cross-system (UC–CSU) AI curriculum or workforce programs. If UCR's bilateral CSU agreement expands or is replicated by other campuses, UC risks developing incoherent cross-system AI education commitments with no systemwide alignment.

### For the committee
Should the committee recommend that any UC campus AI education partnership with CSU or California Community Colleges be coordinated through UCOP — and if so, which office owns that function?
