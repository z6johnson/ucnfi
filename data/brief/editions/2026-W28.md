## item-1 — FTC proposes policy statement targeting companies that suppress accuracy disclosures in AI systems

### What happened
On July 7, 2026, the Federal Trade Commission published a proposed policy statement in the Federal Register asserting that suppressing accuracy information about AI systems — such as performance limitations, error rates, or failure modes — constitutes a deceptive act or practice under Section 5 of the FTC Act. The proposal would apply to any company marketing AI systems commercially and invites public comment.

### Why it matters to UC
UC's systemwide Responsible AI Principles (adopted October 2021) explicitly require that AI methods be explainable and that outcomes be challengeable with meaningful remedies — the 'Transparency' and 'Accuracy, Reliability, Safety' principles. The FTC proposal operationalizes a parallel federal requirement for vendors UC procures from and for any UC-affiliated entities that commercialize AI outputs. UC's Risk Assessment Guide v1.1 flags accuracy and reliability as assessed risk categories, but the guide expressly excludes research and pedagogy from its scope, leaving those domains without a direct accuracy-disclosure standard aligned to this new federal posture.

### For the committee
Should UC amend or extend its Risk Assessment Guide to require vendor accuracy-disclosure attestations — covering research and pedagogical AI uses currently out of scope — before the FTC comment period closes?

## item-2 — DOL proposes adding AI-use questions to the American Time Use Survey — a new federal workforce AI data stream

### What happened
On July 10, 2026, the Department of Labor published a Federal Register notice proposing to add Artificial Intelligence questions to the American Time Use Survey (ATUS) under a Paperwork Reduction Act pre-clearance consultation. If approved, ATUS would become the first recurring federal household survey to capture how workers across occupations and industries allocate time to AI-assisted versus non-AI-assisted tasks.

### Why it matters to UC
UC has a stated 2025 AI Council priority to amplify state and federal AI initiatives for workforce upskilling, and has conducted its own AI adoption survey (264 responses, March 2024 Transparency Report). A permanent federal ATUS AI module would provide longitudinal, nationally representative workforce data UC currently lacks at scale — directly relevant to any UC workforce-upskilling strategy and to the committee's opportunity areas. UC has no systemwide position on how it would use or integrate federal AI workforce data into its own governance or training programs.

### For the committee
Should UC submit comments on the ATUS AI questions proposal to shape what is measured — and designate a committee workstream to align UC workforce-upskilling priorities with the resulting federal data infrastructure?

## item-3 — OpenAI releases GPT-5.6 and 'ChatGPT Work' agent — material capability jump for campuses running ChatGPT Enterprise or Edu

### What happened
On July 9, 2026, OpenAI released GPT-5.6 as the new preferred model in Microsoft 365 Copilot and announced 'ChatGPT Work,' described as an agent capable of taking autonomous action across applications and files for hours on a single goal. GPT-5.6 is positioned as offering stronger performance per token at lower cost. These releases are live, not previews.

### Why it matters to UC
At least three UC campuses — UCLA, UCSF, and UC Davis — hold active ChatGPT Enterprise or ChatGPT Edu agreements; UCM is in active contract negotiations for ChatGPT Edu. The shift to agentic operation in 'ChatGPT Work' is a qualitative change: UC's current Risk Assessment Guide v1.1 was designed for AI-enabled systems in administrative settings and does not address autonomous multi-step agent actions, which introduce new consequential-decision and data-exfiltration risk categories the existing framework does not score. UC's OGC AI Alert prohibits sharing personal information with AI tools without fully executed agreements, but agentic tools that pull data across applications create new exposure pathways not contemplated in the 2024 alert.

### For the committee
Does UC need an emergency addendum to the Risk Assessment Guide — or a new agentic-AI procurement checklist — before campuses with ChatGPT Enterprise agreements begin deploying GPT-5.6 Work agents in administrative workflows?

## item-4 — OpenAI publishes principles for government and national security AI partnerships — directly relevant to UC's three national labs

### What happened
On July 8, 2026, OpenAI published a public statement titled 'Our approach to government and national security partnerships,' articulating principles under which it will work with national governments and security agencies, including commitments to democratic accountability, transparency about capabilities, and refusal of uses that would undermine civilian oversight. The statement explicitly positions OpenAI as a vendor to national security agencies.

### Why it matters to UC
UC manages Lawrence Berkeley, Lawrence Livermore, and Los Alamos National Laboratories — entities that operate under DOE/NNSA governance and are already engaging with AI at the frontier of national security. LANL has a documented partnership with OpenAI (bioscience safety evaluation, July 2024) and a National Security AI Office. LLNL runs El Capitan and has published AI safety research through its Data Science Institute. None of the three labs has a UC-framework AI governance policy in the baseline; all are governed by DOE/NNSA directives. OpenAI's newly stated principles for national security engagements create a vendor-side governance layer that intersects with UC's oversight relationship to the labs — a gap the systemwide baseline records but UC has no current mechanism to address.

### For the committee
Should the committee establish a working group with the national lab members to map where OpenAI's national security partnership principles create obligations or risks that fall inside UC's oversight responsibility — versus entirely within DOE/NNSA jurisdiction?
