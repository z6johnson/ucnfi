## item-1 — GSA opens comment period on first federal LLM data-safeguarding clause for government procurement

### What happened
On June 17, 2026, the General Services Administration published a Federal Register notice soliciting public comment on a draft GSAR clause that would impose baseline data-safeguarding requirements on Large Language Model AI systems acquired by federal agencies. GSA is holding listening sessions before finalizing the clause, signaling an imminent federal procurement standard for LLM data handling. Source: federalregister.gov/documents/2026/06/17/2026-12205.

### Why it matters to UC
UC's existing OGC AI Alert prohibits sharing personal information with AI tools without a fully executed agreement and references IS-3 as the controlling security standard — but that guidance was written for UC's own procurement posture, not to align with a federal clause. If the GSA clause becomes effective, UC contracts on federally funded work may face an additional compliance layer that the current systemwide use policy does not anticipate. UC has a documented applicable-law mapping that tracks federal AI frameworks, but that mapping was last anchored to the Biden AI EO; it does not yet reference any GSA acquisition regulation, creating a gap the committee should flag for OGC and contracting.

### For the committee
Should UCOP submit public comment on the draft GSA LLM clause, and does the systemwide applicable-law mapping need an expedited update to capture federal acquisition requirements before the comment window closes?

## item-2 — OpenAI adds spend controls and usage analytics to ChatGPT Enterprise, directly affecting UCLA and UCSF deployments

### What happened
On June 18, 2026, OpenAI announced new spend controls and usage analytics dashboards for ChatGPT Enterprise, giving organizational administrators granular visibility into token consumption and cost management. The release is aimed at helping enterprises scale AI deployment with tighter budget governance. Source: openai.com/index/chatgpt-enterprise-spend-controls.

### Why it matters to UC
UCLA is the first California university to have deployed ChatGPT Enterprise (September 2024) and UCSF has adopted ChatGPT Enterprise as its primary institutional LLM, replacing the Versa Chat platform. The new controls are operationally significant for both: UCLA's DTS manages a multi-tier cost model (Core at $67.80/yr, Advanced at $0.05/credit) and UCSF allows P3 and P4 data in ChatGPT Enterprise — cost overruns or unexpected usage patterns at those data-sensitivity levels carry both financial and compliance risk. UC has no systemwide enterprise AI platform, so each campus negotiates and administers its OpenAI relationship independently, meaning the benefit of these new controls accrues unevenly across the system.

### For the committee
Should the committee recommend that campuses using ChatGPT Enterprise report usage and cost data through a common systemwide dashboard, or is campus-level autonomy in contract administration the appropriate posture?

## item-4 — OpenAI publishes deployment simulation method for pre-release safety evaluation, creating new due-diligence benchmark for AI procurement

### What happened
On June 16, 2026, OpenAI released details of its Deployment Simulation methodology, which uses real conversation data to predict model behavior before release and improve safety evaluation accuracy. The method is positioned as a scalable pre-deployment safety testing approach for frontier models. Source: openai.com/index/deployment-simulation.

### Why it matters to UC
UC's systemwide Risk Assessment Guide (v1.1) establishes a two-phase risk framework covering procurement, development, and deployment of AI-enabled systems — including scrutiny of third-party models trained on bulk data — but it does not specify what vendor-side safety evaluation evidence UC should require before approving a new model or model-version for use. With multiple campuses holding active OpenAI contracts (UCLA, UCSF, UC San Diego), the emergence of a named, documented vendor safety methodology like Deployment Simulation creates a concrete artifact that UC procurement and compliance reviewers could reference — or require — as part of Phase 1 or Phase 2 risk assessment. The Risk Assessment Guide currently leaves vendor safety-testing standards to each campus's own approval process.

### For the committee
Should the committee recommend that the Risk Assessment Guide (v1.1) be updated to specify minimum vendor safety-evaluation evidence requirements — such as deployment simulation documentation — as a condition of campus approval for high-impact AI tool use?
