## item-1 — FAR overhaul proposes to strip contractor ethics and termination provisions, reshaping the federal floor for AI vendor accountability

### What happened
The FAR Council (OFPP, DoD, GSA, and NASA) published a proposed rule implementing E.O. 14275 to eliminate what the administration calls excessive acquisition regulations from FAR Parts 3 (ethics and conflicts of interest) and 49 (contract termination). The rule would reduce federal due-diligence requirements for government contractors — a category that includes AI vendors serving federally-funded university research and operations.

### Why it matters to UC
UC's AI Risk Assessment Guide v1.1 (ucop-07a) explicitly governs AI in procurement, development, and deployment of AI-enabled systems across all campuses. If federal contractor ethics and conflict-of-interest requirements are weakened, UC's internal risk framework becomes a more consequential backstop — but it currently applies only to administrative AI settings and explicitly excludes research and pedagogy, leaving a documented scope gap precisely where federal oversight is also retreating.

### For the committee
Does UC's AI procurement risk framework need to be formally affirmed as the minimum standard for AI vendor accountability regardless of changes to federal acquisition rules, and should its explicit exclusion of research and pedagogy settings be revisited?

## item-2 — OpenAI previews GPT-5.6 Sol — advanced cybersecurity and science model — requiring contract and data-security review at campuses holding ChatGPT Enterprise

### What happened
OpenAI announced GPT-5.6 Sol, a next-generation model with substantially stronger capabilities in coding, science, and cybersecurity, paired with what the company describes as its most advanced safety stack. UCLA and UCSF currently operate ChatGPT Enterprise under campus-level contracts; UC Davis is implementing ChatGPT Edu. New model generations typically trigger updated terms of service and data handling provisions.

### Why it matters to UC
UC's OGC AI Alert prohibits sharing personal information with AI tools without a fully executed agreement covering UC data, and the Risk Assessment Guide v1.1 requires elevated scrutiny for third-party models trained on or accessing bulk data. These controls sit at the campus level — the baseline documents that no systemwide enterprise AI platform exists and campuses manage vendor contracts individually, meaning GPT-5.6 Sol's arrival will be reviewed (or not) on three separate timelines with no systemwide coordination mechanism.

### For the committee
Should the committee recommend a standing systemwide protocol requiring campuses to notify UCOP and trigger a coordinated data-security review whenever a contracted AI vendor releases a materially new model generation?

## item-3 — OpenAI research confirms AI agents now executing complex multi-step professional tasks — a deployment category outside UC's current risk governance scope

### What happened
OpenAI published a research paper demonstrating that AI agents are completing longer, more complex tasks across professional roles — moving from single-query AI use toward autonomous multi-step workflow execution. The paper frames this as a fundamental shift in how AI transforms productivity across organizational functions.

### Why it matters to UC
UC's AI Risk Assessment Guide v1.1 explicitly limits its scope to AI in administrative settings and states it does NOT cover research or pedagogy. Agentic AI that operates across research workflows, course delivery, and student-facing advising falls outside the framework's boundary by design. Three campuses (UCLA, UCSD, UCI) have active enterprise platforms through which agents could be deployed today without a governing risk process at the systemwide level.

### For the committee
Should the committee commission a targeted extension of the Risk Assessment Guide to address agentic AI deployments in research and teaching contexts, before campus adoption of agent-capable tools outpaces governance coverage?

## item-4 — OpenAI joins Appia Foundation to build shared global AI evaluation and safety standards — UC's 2021 principles have not been publicly reviewed against the emerging consensus

### What happened
OpenAI announced support for the Appia Foundation's effort to develop shared evaluation frameworks, safety practices, and international cooperation on AI standards. The initiative aims to produce convergent global benchmarks that could become reference standards for AI vendors, governments, and institutions.

### Why it matters to UC
UC adopted 8 Responsible AI Principles in October 2021 and has since mapped them against the EU AI Act, NIST AI RMF, WHO guidance, and other frameworks (ucop-13). However, the baseline records no documented process for periodically reviewing UC's principles against evolving external standards, and the emerging Appia-led consensus represents a significant new reference point that post-dates UC's mapping work. UC's principles are widely cited across campus policies system-wide, so drift between them and industry-adopted standards has downstream governance implications.

### For the committee
Should the committee establish a standing review cycle for UC's Responsible AI Principles — including explicit comparison against emerging industry and international standards — and designate a body responsible for recommending updates to the President?
