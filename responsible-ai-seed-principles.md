# Responsible AI Seed Principles

Guidelines for how our applications handle data, make decisions, and interact with the people who use them.

## Appropriateness

Not every problem needs an AI-powered solution. Before building, evaluate whether the potential benefit to the people affected justifies the risk, complexity, and cost of applying AI — and whether a simpler approach would serve them better. Some contexts warrant prohibition, not optimization.

## Transparency

People should know when they're interacting with an AI-enabled system. The methods behind AI-driven outcomes should be explainable to the degree possible, and users should have clear paths to understand how a result was reached, challenge it if they disagree, and access meaningful remedies if they're harmed.

## Accuracy, Reliability, and Safety

AI-enabled features must be effective and reliable for their intended use — not just at launch, but across their entire lifetime. This means verifiable accuracy, ongoing monitoring, and a commitment to safe and secure operation as conditions change.

## Fairness and Non-Discrimination

Every AI-enabled system should be assessed for bias and discrimination — not once, but as a continuous practice. Procedures for identifying, mitigating, and remedying these harms should be built into the development process, not bolted on after the fact.

## Privacy and Security

Design for maximum privacy and security of persons and personal data. This is a constraint that shapes architecture, not a compliance checkbox applied at the end. Data minimization, access controls, and user consent are structural decisions, not policy footnotes.

## Human Values

AI-enabled tools should support human agency and dignity. People retain the right to understand, question, and override automated decisions that affect them. Civil and human rights implications must be examined whenever AI is introduced into contexts where those rights could be compromised.

## Shared Benefit

The benefits of AI should be distributed equitably — socially, economically, and environmentally. Systems designed to serve some users well at the expense of others fail this principle regardless of their technical performance.

## Accountability

We are accountable for the systems we build and deploy. That accountability is not diffused by the complexity of the technology or the involvement of third-party tools. It flows from these principles and applies to every stage of development, deployment, and ongoing operation.
